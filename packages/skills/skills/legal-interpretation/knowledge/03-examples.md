## Worked examples

The following examples illustrate how the three-component framework and reasoning structures operate in practice. They are drawn from canonical problems in legal interpretation across different traditions.

### Example 1: Hart's "no vehicles in the park"

**The rule:** "No vehicles shall be permitted in the park."

**Issue:** Does the rule prohibit (a) a war memorial tank mounted on a plinth, (b) a child's battery-powered toy car, (c) a motorized wheelchair?

**Analysis using the three components:**

_Language._ "Vehicle" in its ordinary meaning denotes a conveyance used for transporting people or goods. The semantic range is broad: it clearly covers cars and trucks; its boundaries become uncertain with toy cars, wheelchairs, and stationary display objects. The tank on a plinth is not functioning as a vehicle (it is not transporting anything); the toy car is a miniature that barely qualifies; the wheelchair is a mobility aid. Language alone does not resolve these cases; it establishes the zone of contestation.

_Purpose._ Subjective purpose: the legislature likely intended to preserve the park's tranquility and safety by excluding traffic. Objective purpose: a reasonable legislator would aim to protect the park's recreational function. Both purposes suggest that the mischief targeted is noise, danger, and disruption from motorized traffic, not the physical presence of anything that could technically be called a "vehicle."

_Normative context._ If the jurisdiction's human rights framework protects the right to mobility for persons with disabilities, excluding wheelchairs would raise proportionality concerns. Higher-law conformity favors a reading that permits wheelchairs. No collision rules are triggered for the other cases.

**Conclusions:**

- _Tank on plinth:_ Not prohibited. Although a tank is semantically a vehicle, its use here (stationary display) falls outside the purpose of the rule. Language can bear the exclusion; purpose demands it.
- _Toy car:_ Not prohibited. At the margins of the semantic range, purpose resolves the vagueness: a toy car poses no danger or disruption.
- _Motorized wheelchair:_ Not prohibited. Purpose (safety and tranquility) is not served by exclusion, and normative context (disability rights) independently supports this reading.

This example demonstrates that language sets the boundaries, but purpose and normative context determine the result within those boundaries.

### Example 2: Lenaerts and the ECJ's interpretive methods

The European Court of Justice's interpretive practice illustrates the three components operating in a supranational legal order with distinctive characteristics: multilingual authenticity, teleological primacy, and the principle of effet utile.

#### Multilingual reconciliation

**Problem:** Article 8 of Regulation (EC) No 261/2004 grants passengers the right to "reimbursement" (English), "remboursement" (French), and "Erstattung" (German) when their flight is cancelled. Does this include compensation for consequential losses (hotel costs, missed connections)?

**Analysis:** The language component requires comparing all authentic versions. All three terms denote a return of the price paid; none naturally extends to consequential damages. Where versions converge, the shared meaning controls. Purpose (both subjective, from the regulation's recitals emphasizing a "high level of protection," and objective, the regulation's consumer-protection function) might press toward a broader reading, but language sets the outer boundary. The conclusion: "reimbursement" covers the ticket price and equivalent transport, not consequential losses; those require a separate legal basis.

#### Effet utile and teleological method

**Problem:** Article 157 TFEU provides that "each Member State shall ensure that the principle of equal pay for male and female workers for equal work or work of equal value is applied." Does this provision have direct horizontal effect (i.e., can it be invoked by one private party against another)?

**Analysis:** Language: "each Member State shall ensure" suggests a state obligation, not a rule directly binding private employers. Purpose: the provision's objective purpose (eliminating gender discrimination in pay, a fundamental value of the EU legal order) would be undermined if it could only be enforced against the state. Normative context: the principle of effet utile requires that Treaty provisions be given their fullest practical effect. The ECJ in _Defrenne v Sabena_ (Case 43/75) held that Article 157 has direct horizontal effect, because limiting it to state action would deprive it of practical effectiveness. Here, normative context (effet utile) and objective purpose together override the most natural linguistic reading.

#### Contract interpretation: hypothetical

**Problem:** A software licensing agreement provides that the licensee may use the software "on its premises." The licensee's employees begin working remotely. Does the license cover remote use?

**Analysis:** Language: "on its premises" ordinarily refers to a physical location controlled by the licensee. The phrase is vague as applied to remote work, but it is not ambiguous; it simply did not contemplate this scenario. Purpose: subjective purpose (the parties' intent at the time of contracting) likely contemplated on-site use, since the contract predated widespread remote work. Objective purpose (a reasonable licensor-licensee arrangement) would aim to ensure the software is used by authorized personnel, not to restrict the physical location of use. Normative context: good faith and the principle of practical consequences favor an interpretation that permits remote use by the same authorized employees.

The example illustrates how the three components interact when a text encounters circumstances its authors did not foresee: language sets a boundary that appears restrictive, but purpose and normative context may justify a reading that adapts the text to changed conditions, so long as the semantic boundary is not exceeded.
