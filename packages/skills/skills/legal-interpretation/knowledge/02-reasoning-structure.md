## Reasoning structures for legal interpretation

Legal interpretation is an act of argumentation: the interpreter must justify the chosen meaning of a text. The following structures provide the scaffolding for that justification. They are not alternatives to the three-component framework; they are the forms in which the framework's results are expressed.

### IRAC (Issue, Rule, Application, Conclusion)

IRAC is the standard analytical structure for legal reasoning in common law traditions. Each interpretive question should be addressed through:

1. **Issue.** State the precise interpretive question. Frame it as narrowly as possible: "Does 'vehicle' in Section 3(1) include electric scooters?" rather than "What does the statute mean?"
2. **Rule.** Identify the applicable interpretive principles. Draw from the three components: which linguistic canons apply? What does the legislative history reveal about purpose? What higher-law norms constrain the reading?
3. **Application.** Apply the identified principles to the specific text. This is where the interpretive work happens: trace how each component (language, purpose, normative context) bears on the question, note where they converge or conflict, and explain the weight given to each.
4. **Conclusion.** State the result and the degree of confidence. Where components conflict, explain which prevails for this type of text and why.

### Toulmin model of argumentation

The Toulmin model provides a more granular structure for individual interpretive arguments:

- **Claim.** The proposed interpretation (e.g. "'vehicle' in this context includes electric scooters").
- **Data (grounds).** The evidence supporting the claim (e.g. the statutory text, legislative history, comparable provisions).
- **Warrant.** The interpretive principle that connects data to claim (e.g. "ordinary meaning presumption," "ejusdem generis," "teleological interpretation").
- **Backing.** The authority or rationale supporting the warrant itself (e.g. case law applying the same canon, scholarly authority, the legal tradition's hierarchy of interpretive methods).
- **Qualifier.** The degree of certainty ("the text clearly supports," "on balance," "arguably," "the better reading is").
- **Rebuttal.** The conditions under which the claim would fail or an alternative interpretation would prevail. Every honest interpretive argument must acknowledge the strongest counter-reading.

Use the Toulmin model when a single interpretive point requires detailed justification, particularly when arguing against a plausible alternative reading.

### Alexy's balancing framework

When interpretation involves competing fundamental rights or principles, Robert Alexy's proportionality framework structures the analysis:

1. **Suitability.** Does the proposed interpretation advance the legitimate aim it is said to serve?
2. **Necessity.** Is there an alternative interpretation that would achieve the same aim with less restriction on competing rights or principles?
3. **Proportionality in the strict sense (balancing).** Does the importance of the aim, and the degree to which the interpretation advances it, outweigh the severity of the interference with competing rights?

This framework applies most directly when interpreting rights-limiting provisions (constitutional or statutory restrictions on fundamental rights), but the balancing logic extends to any situation where interpretive choices implicate competing normative values.

### Integrating reasoning structures with the three-component framework

The three components (language, purpose, normative context) generate the _substance_ of the interpretive argument. The reasoning structures provide the _form_:

- Use **IRAC** as the top-level organization for each interpretive question.
- Within the Application step, use the **Toulmin model** to structure individual arguments, especially where the interpretation is contested.
- When competing rights or principles are at stake, embed **Alexy's balancing** within the normative context analysis.

The interpreter's task is not merely to state a conclusion but to make the path from text to meaning transparent, replicable, and open to critique.
