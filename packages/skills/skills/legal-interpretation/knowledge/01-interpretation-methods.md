## The three components of interpretation

Every act of legal interpretation involves three components that work together, not in sequence. You must consider all three, regardless of the legal system.

### 1. Language: the semantic boundaries

Language defines the range of meanings a legal text can bear. Interpretation must remain within these boundaries; going beyond what the text can semantically support is not interpretation but the creation of a new norm.

#### Principles

- **Ordinary meaning presumption.** Prefer the natural and ordinary meaning of terms as understood by the text's target audience, unless a statutory or contractual definition applies, or the text uses a recognized technical term of art.
- **Context determines meaning.** A word's meaning is shaped by its textual surroundings: the clause, the section, the instrument as a whole. Read words in their internal context (surrounding provisions) and external context (the state of the law, the circumstances at the time of creation).
- **Terminological consistency.** The same term within the same instrument should carry the same meaning. Conversely, different terms should denote different concepts.
- **Explicit and implicit meaning.** A text communicates both what it says directly and what it implies through logic, structure, and context. Implicit meaning is part of the text's semantic content, not an addition to it.
- **Ambiguity and vagueness.** Distinguish between ambiguity (a word or sentence has more than one possible meaning) and vagueness (the boundaries of a concept are uncertain). Ambiguity can often be resolved through context; vagueness requires purpose-based reasoning.
- **Linguistic canons.** Use standard linguistic canons as tools for establishing semantic range, not as rules that determine legal meaning:
  - _noscitur a sociis_: a word is known by the company it keeps.
  - _ejusdem generis_: a general term following a list of specifics is limited to items of the same kind.
  - _expressio unius est exclusio alterius_: the express mention of one thing may imply the exclusion of others, but consider whether the enumeration is intended to be exhaustive.
- **Private and public language.** In private texts (contracts, wills), the parties may establish their own definitions; the interpreter should consider both private and public meanings, choosing according to the type of text and the weight given to subjective intent. In public texts (statutes, constitutions), the text must be understandable in its public language.
- **Multilingual instruments.** Where a legal text has equally authentic language versions (e.g. EU law, international treaties), no single version controls. Divergences among versions must be resolved through contextual and purpose-based interpretation.

### 2. Purpose: subjective and objective

The core of interpretation is determining the purpose the text is designed to realize. Purpose has two foundations: the subjective purpose (the author's actual intent) and the objective purpose (the values and goals of the legal system). Both apply simultaneously to every legal text.

#### Subjective purpose (authorial intent)

The subjective purpose is the historical intent of the text's author, encompassing the values, goals, and policies the author sought to actualize when creating the text. It is a historical fact, fixed at the time of the text's creation.

#### Principles

- **Sources of subjective intent.** Learn the author's intent from the language of the text itself as well as from external sources: explanatory memoranda, legislative history, travaux préparatoires, committee reports, preambles, recitals, official commentary, or (for contracts) negotiation records and prior correspondence.
- **Textual sources carry greater weight.** Intent inferred from the text's own language is generally more reliable than intent derived from extrinsic sources. Extrinsic evidence supplements the text; it does not replace it.
- **Rejected alternatives.** If the author considered and rejected an alternative formulation, the rejected alternative should not be achieved through interpretation.
- **Multiple purposes.** A text typically serves multiple purposes at different levels of abstraction. Distinguish between horizontal purposes (multiple goals at the same level) and vertical purposes (ascending from specific objectives to general values). A specific purpose should be preferred over a general one when they conflict at the same level of abstraction, but interpret specific purposes in light of the general ones.
- **Collective authorship.** For texts created by collective bodies (legislatures, constitutional conventions), subjective purpose is the shared intent of the body, not the private views of individual members. Statements by individual legislators are evidence of, but not equivalent to, the collective intent.

#### Objective purpose (systemic values)

The objective purpose is what a reasonable author would have intended in the circumstances, and what the legal system's fundamental values demand. Unlike subjective purpose, it is not fixed in time; it reflects the values of the system as they exist at the time of interpretation.

#### Principles

- **Reasonable-author standard.** At the most concrete level, ask: what would a reasonable person in the author's position have intended this text to achieve?
- **Type-based purpose.** At a higher level of abstraction, consider the purpose typical of this kind of legal arrangement, i.e. the function that this type of contract, statute, or constitutional provision characteristically serves.
- **Fundamental values.** At the highest level, the objective purpose actualizes the fundamental values of the legal system: democracy, rule of law, separation of powers, human rights, equality, justice, good faith, proportionality, and legal certainty. These values form a "normative umbrella" that extends to all legal texts within the system.
- **Harmony with the legal order.** The text is a creature of its legal system. Interpretation should seek to create harmony between the individual text and the system that surrounds it, so that each text functions coherently within the broader normative framework.
- **Practical consequences.** Prefer the interpretation that produces reasonable, workable outcomes. Avoid readings that lead to absurd, unjust, or unworkable results.

#### Balancing subjective and objective purpose

The weight given to subjective versus objective purpose varies by the type of text. There is no universal fixed hierarchy; instead, the balance is calibrated:

| Text type                      | Subjective purpose                                                                | Objective purpose                                                                                           |
| ------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Wills                          | Dominant: the testator's intent is the primary guide                              | Supplementary; fills gaps when intent is unclear                                                            |
| Contracts                      | Weighs heavily (joint intent of the parties)                                      | Supplements; gains weight in adhesion contracts, consumer contracts, and where subjective intent is unknown |
| Specific/new statutes          | Significant: the legislative intent is fresh and accessible                       | Important, but typically confirms subjective purpose                                                        |
| General/old statutes and codes | Supplementary: historical intent is distant and may not address modern conditions | Dominant: the text must serve contemporary needs                                                            |
| Constitutions                  | Secondary: the founders' intent informs but does not control                      | Dominant: a constitution must serve the evolving needs of society and its fundamental values                |
| International treaties         | Relevant (object and purpose at the time of conclusion)                           | Important; guided by good faith, context, and subsequent practice (VCLT Arts. 31-33)                        |

When subjective and objective purpose converge, the interpretation can be stated with high confidence. When they conflict, the resolution depends on the type of text and the weight the legal system assigns to each component.

### 3. Normative context: the text within its legal system

A legal text does not exist in isolation. It operates within a structured normative environment that constrains and informs its meaning. The legal order is presumed to be consistent, non-contradictory, and non-redundant.

#### Principles

- **No provision is redundant.** Every provision is presumed to have independent meaning and legal effect (effet utile). An interpretation that renders a provision meaningless should be rejected.
- **Higher-law conformity.** Prefer the interpretation compatible with the constitutional order, bill of rights, or equivalent higher-law norms. Where a text can be read in a way that is constitutionally valid and a way that is not, prefer the valid reading.
- **Supranational and international obligations.** Where the state is bound by supranational law (e.g. EU directives, ECHR) or international treaties, read domestic law, as far as possible, in light of the wording and purpose of the governing instrument.
- **Collision rules.** Where norms genuinely conflict after all interpretive possibilities are exhausted:
  - _lex superior derogat legi inferiori_: a higher-ranking norm prevails over a lower-ranking one.
  - _lex specialis derogat legi generali_: a specific norm prevails over a general one covering the same subject.
  - _lex posterior derogat legi priori_: a later norm prevails over an earlier one of equal rank (but a later general norm does not automatically override an earlier specific one).
- **Structural arguments.** Consider the heading, title, and placement of the provision within the structure of the act (argument a rubrica). The systematic position of a provision is evidence of its purpose.
- **Logical arguments.** Use arguments a contrario (from the opposite), a fortiori (from the stronger case), per analogiam (by analogy), and reductio ad absurdum (rejection of interpretations that lead to absurd outcomes). Make the reasoning transparent and explain which argument form is being used and why.
- **Proportionality.** Where an interpretation implicates fundamental rights, apply the proportionality analysis recognized in the relevant jurisdiction (e.g. legitimate aim, suitability, necessity, proportionality in the strict sense).
- **Established case law and scholarly opinion.** Like cases should be treated alike. Consider prevailing judicial interpretations and scholarly consensus as evidence of how the text has been understood within the legal community, while noting that prior interpretations are not immune from revision.
