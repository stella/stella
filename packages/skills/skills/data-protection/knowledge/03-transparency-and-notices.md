## Transparency and privacy notices

Transparency is both a standalone principle (Art 5(1)(a)) and
an operational obligation implemented through Articles 12-14.
The EDPB's Guidelines on Transparency (WP260 rev.01) establish
the qualitative standard: information must be concise,
transparent, intelligible, easily accessible, and in clear and
plain language.

### Article 13: direct collection

When personal data is collected directly from the data subject,
the controller must provide the following at the time of
collection:

| Element                                 | Article      | Notes                                                                                                                               |
| --------------------------------------- | ------------ | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Controller identity and contact details | 13(1)(a)     | Include DPO contact details where applicable (Art 37)                                                                               |
| Purposes and lawful basis               | 13(1)(c)     | State each purpose and its corresponding lawful basis                                                                               |
| Legitimate interests pursued            | 13(1)(d)     | Required only where Art 6(1)(f) is the basis; must be specific, not generic                                                         |
| Recipients or categories of recipients  | 13(1)(e)     | Meaningful categories, not "third parties as necessary"                                                                             |
| International transfers and safeguards  | 13(1)(f)     | Identify the transfer mechanism (adequacy, SCCs, BCRs)                                                                              |
| Retention period or criteria            | 13(2)(a)     | Specific periods per purpose; "as long as necessary" alone is insufficient                                                          |
| Data subject rights                     | 13(2)(b)-(d) | Right of access, rectification, erasure, restriction, portability, objection; right to withdraw consent; right to lodge a complaint |
| Statutory or contractual requirement    | 13(2)(e)     | Whether provision of data is obligatory and the consequences of not providing it                                                    |
| Automated decision-making               | 13(2)(f)     | If Art 22 applies: meaningful information about the logic, significance, and envisaged consequences                                 |

### Article 14: indirect collection

Where data is not obtained directly from the data subject,
Art 14 adds two requirements: the categories of personal data
concerned (Art 14(1)(d)) and the source from which the data
originates, including whether it came from publicly accessible
sources (Art 14(2)(f)). The information must be provided within
a reasonable period (maximum one month) or at first
communication with the data subject, whichever is earlier
(Art 14(3)).

### Privacy notice review method

1. **Determine the collection context**: direct (Art 13) or
   indirect (Art 14) collection, or both. This determines the
   applicable checklist.
2. **Map requirements to notice content**: check each mandatory
   element against the notice text. Record whether the element
   is present, absent, or partially addressed.
3. **Assess completeness**: identify missing elements and
   evaluate their materiality (an absent retention period is
   more significant than an absent DPO email if the DPO is
   named elsewhere).
4. **Assess quality**: evaluate whether present elements meet
   the transparency standard:
   - _Conciseness_: is the notice proportionate to the
     processing described?
   - _Intelligibility_: is it written for the actual audience
     (consumers vs. business counterparties)?
   - _Accessibility_: is it presented at the right time and
     place (layered notices, just-in-time notices)?
   - _Specificity_: does it describe the actual processing,
     or does it use vague, catch-all language?
5. **Check for misleading framing**: identify language that is
   technically accurate but creates a false impression (e.g.,
   "we do not sell your data" when third-party sharing achieves
   the same effect through other contractual structures).

### Quality dimensions

#### Specificity

Vague formulations to flag:

- "We may use your data for marketing purposes" (which data,
  which marketing, which channels?)
- "We share data with our partners" (which partners, for what
  purposes, under what basis?)
- "We retain data as long as necessary" (necessary for what
  purpose, and what determines the period?)

#### Layering

The EDPB recommends layered notices for complex processing:
a short first layer with the most important information
(controller identity, purposes, rights) and a detailed second
layer with the full Art 13/14 content. Assess whether the
layers are properly linked and whether the first layer is
genuinely informative rather than merely a link to the full
notice.

### Common gaps and red flags

- Blanket consent for unspecified future purposes
- Retention periods stated only as "in accordance with our
  retention policy" without specifying the policy's content
  or making it accessible
- Legitimate interests claimed without specifying the interest
- International transfers disclosed without identifying the
  transfer mechanism or the recipient countries
- Notice does not distinguish between different processing
  activities that serve different purposes
- Cookie/tracking notice separated from the privacy notice
  with no cross-reference, creating an incomplete picture
