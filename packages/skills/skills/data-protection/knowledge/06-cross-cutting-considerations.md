## Cross-cutting considerations

The following issues span multiple assessment types. Apply
them whenever the analysis triggers the relevant conditions.

### International transfers (Chapter V)

Personal data may only be transferred outside the EEA where
an adequate level of protection is ensured. The transfer
pathway follows this sequence:

1. **Adequacy decision** (Art 45): the Commission has determined
   that the third country or international organisation ensures
   an adequate level of protection. Currently applicable
   decisions include those for the UK, Japan, Republic of Korea,
   and the EU-US Data Privacy Framework, among others.
2. **Appropriate safeguards** (Art 46): in the absence of an
   adequacy decision, transfers may proceed subject to
   safeguards including standard contractual clauses (SCCs)
   adopted by the Commission (Decision 2021/914), binding
   corporate rules (Art 47), or codes of conduct and
   certification mechanisms.
3. **Derogations** (Art 49): in the absence of both adequacy
   and appropriate safeguards, transfers are permitted only on
   limited grounds (explicit consent, contractual necessity,
   important public interest, legal claims, vital interests,
   public registers).

#### Post-Schrems II obligations

Following _Schrems II_ (C-311/18), controllers relying on
SCCs must assess whether the law and practice of the recipient
country provide an essentially equivalent level of protection.
The EDPB's Recommendations 01/2020 set out a six-step process:
identify transfers; verify the transfer tool; assess whether
the tool is effective in light of the recipient country's legal
framework; adopt supplementary measures if necessary; take
procedural steps required by the supplementary measures;
re-evaluate at appropriate intervals. The EDPB's
Recommendations 02/2020 on European Essential Guarantees
provide criteria for assessing whether a third country's
surveillance framework meets the standard of essential
equivalence.

Note that the EDPB interprets "transfer" broadly: remote access
to personal data from a third country (e.g., a support team
accessing EEA data from outside the EEA) constitutes a transfer
subject to Chapter V requirements.

### Accountability in practice

Article 5(2) requires the controller to demonstrate compliance
with the principles. The accountability obligation is not
abstract; it manifests differently in each assessment context:

| Assessment type            | Accountability evidence                                                                         |
| -------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Processing activity review | Records of processing (Art 30), DPIA where required (Art 35), documented lawful basis selection |
| Privacy notice review      | Versioned notices, evidence of delivery or accessibility, layered notice strategy               |
| DPA review                 | Executed DPA with all Art 28(3) elements, sub-processor register, audit reports                 |
| Breach assessment          | Breach register (Art 33(5)), notification records, post-incident review documentation           |

### Supervisory authority guidance

EDPB guidelines, opinions, and recommendations represent the
collective position of EEA supervisory authorities. While not
legislation, they carry significant practical authority:
controllers who follow EDPB guidance are unlikely to face
enforcement action on the points addressed.

National supervisory authority guidance may go further or
address member-state-specific requirements. Where a specific
jurisdiction is identified, check for relevant national
guidance in addition to EDPB positions.

### Member state variation

The GDPR permits member state divergence in several areas.
Flag these when the analysis touches them:

- Employee data processing (opening clauses in Art 88)
- Age of consent for information society services (Art 8(1):
  member states may lower from 16 to 13)
- Health data processing conditions (Art 9(2)(h)-(j))
- National security and law enforcement processing (outside
  GDPR scope; Directive (EU) 2016/680 applies)
- Freedom of expression and information (Art 85)
- National DPIA lists under Art 35(4)

### Interaction with other regulatory frameworks

- **ePrivacy Directive** (2002/58/EC as amended): governs
  electronic communications, cookies, and direct marketing.
  The ePrivacy rules apply as lex specialis to the GDPR for
  their specific subject matter.
- **AI Act** (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689): where processing
  involves AI systems, the AI Act's requirements (risk
  classification, transparency, human oversight) apply alongside
  the GDPR. Data protection impact assessments should address
  both frameworks where applicable.
- **NIS2 Directive** (Directive (EU) 2022/2555): imposes
  cybersecurity and incident reporting obligations on essential
  and important entities. NIS2 incident notifications may
  overlap with GDPR breach notifications where a security
  incident also involves personal data.
- **Sector-specific rules**: financial services (PSD2, MiFID II),
  health (Clinical Trials Regulation), telecommunications
  (EECC): these may impose additional data processing
  requirements or conditions that interact with the GDPR.

### Proportionality and the risk-based approach

The GDPR's obligations are calibrated to risk. Articles 24, 25,
and 32 require measures "appropriate to the risk" of the
processing. This means:

- Low-risk, routine processing (e.g., employee contact
  directories) requires baseline compliance but not the full
  apparatus of DPIAs and extensive safeguards.
- High-risk processing (large-scale profiling, systematic
  monitoring, sensitive data at scale) requires a DPIA, enhanced
  safeguards, and potentially prior consultation with the
  supervisory authority (Art 36).

Calibrate the depth and detail of each assessment to the risk
profile of the processing under review.
