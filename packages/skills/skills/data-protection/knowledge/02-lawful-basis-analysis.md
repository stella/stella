## Lawful basis analysis

Every instance of processing personal data requires a lawful
basis under Article 6(1). The basis must be identified before
processing begins and cannot be changed retrospectively
(EDPB Guidelines 2/2019 on Art 6(1)(b), para 18).

### The six bases

| Basis                                 | Article | Key conditions                                                                                                                          |
| ------------------------------------- | ------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Consent                               | 6(1)(a) | Freely given, specific, informed, unambiguous; demonstrable (Art 7); withdrawable at any time without detriment                         |
| Contractual necessity                 | 6(1)(b) | Processing objectively necessary to perform or enter a contract with the data subject; not merely useful or permitted by the contract   |
| Legal obligation                      | 6(1)(c) | An obligation imposed by EU or member state law to which the controller is subject; must be sufficiently clear and precise              |
| Vital interests                       | 6(1)(d) | Necessary to protect the vital interests (life-threatening situations) of the data subject or another person; narrow scope, last resort |
| Public interest or official authority | 6(1)(e) | Processing necessary for a task carried out in the public interest or in the exercise of official authority vested in the controller    |
| Legitimate interests                  | 6(1)(f) | Three-part balancing test (see below); not available for processing by public authorities in the performance of their tasks             |

### Lawful basis selection method

1. Characterise the processing: what personal data, whose, for
   what purpose, by what means.
2. Eliminate inapplicable bases: vital interests applies only
   to life-threatening situations; public interest/official
   authority applies only where vested by law; legal obligation
   requires an identifiable legal provision.
3. Assess consent viability: evaluate whether consent can be
   freely given (power imbalance, conditionality, granularity).
   If the controller cannot accept a refusal without adverse
   consequences to the data subject, consent is not freely given
   (EDPB Guidelines 5/2020 on consent, para 13).
4. Assess contractual necessity: the CJEU in _Huber_ (C-524/06)
   required objective necessity, not mere contractual permission.
   Ask whether the processing is genuinely required to fulfil the
   contract's core obligations, not ancillary purposes.
5. If consent and contractual necessity are unavailable or
   inappropriate, assess legitimate interests using the
   three-part test below.

### Legitimate interests: three-part test

The test originates from the CJEU's interpretation of
Art 7(f) of Directive 95/46 (now Art 6(1)(f) GDPR) and was
elaborated by the Article 29 Working Party (Opinion 06/2014):

1. **Identify the legitimate interest**: a concrete, lawful
   interest pursued by the controller or a third party (not
   merely speculative or hypothetical).
2. **Necessity**: the processing must be necessary to achieve
   the interest; if the purpose can be achieved by less
   intrusive means, the processing fails the necessity limb.
3. **Balancing**: weigh the controller's interest against the
   data subject's interests, rights, and freedoms.

#### Balancing factors

- Nature of the personal data (special categories carry
  greater weight for the data subject)
- Whether the data derives from publicly accessible sources
  (relevant but not dispositive; _TK v Asociația_, C-708/18)
- Reasonable expectations of the data subject based on the
  relationship with the controller (Recital 47)
- Status of the data subject (children warrant heightened
  protection, Art 6(1)(f) final sentence)
- Impact of the processing on the data subject (severity,
  likelihood, reversibility)
- Modalities of processing: how the data is used, combined,
  or made accessible to others (_TK_, C-708/18)
- Safeguards adopted by the controller to mitigate the impact
- Whether a qualified opt-out mechanism is available

### Special category data: two-gate analysis

Processing special category data (Art 9(1): racial or ethnic
origin, political opinions, religious or philosophical beliefs,
trade union membership, genetic data, biometric data for
identification, health data, sex life or sexual orientation)
requires satisfying both gates:

1. **Gate 1 (Art 6)**: a valid lawful basis under Art 6(1).
2. **Gate 2 (Art 9(2))**: an additional exception lifting the
   Art 9(1) prohibition (e.g., explicit consent under Art 9(2)(a),
   employment law under Art 9(2)(b), substantial public interest
   under Art 9(2)(g)).

Both gates must be satisfied simultaneously; satisfying one
without the other is insufficient.

### DPIA trigger assessment

Article 35(1) requires a data protection impact assessment
where processing is "likely to result in a high risk" to
data subjects' rights and freedoms. The EDPB's Guidelines on
DPIAs (WP248 rev.01) identify nine criteria; processing that
meets two or more generally requires a DPIA:

1. Evaluation or scoring (including profiling)
2. Automated decision-making with legal or significant effects
3. Systematic monitoring
4. Sensitive data or data of a highly personal nature
5. Data processed on a large scale
6. Matching or combining datasets
7. Data concerning vulnerable data subjects
8. Innovative use or applying new technological solutions
9. Processing that prevents data subjects from exercising a right
   or using a service or contract

Supervisory authorities also publish national DPIA lists
under Art 35(4); check the relevant authority's list when a
specific jurisdiction is identified.
