## GDPR structural framework

The General Data Protection Regulation (Regulation (EU) 2016/679)
is organised in eleven chapters. The analytical structure below
maps the regulation's architecture to the assessment tasks this
skill performs.

### Core concepts

#### Personal data

Any information relating to an identified or identifiable natural
person (Art 4(1)). Identifiability is assessed by considering all
means reasonably likely to be used, including by third parties
(Recital 26). The CJEU has interpreted this broadly: dynamic IP
addresses can constitute personal data where the controller has
legal means to obtain identifying information from a third party
(_Breyer_, C-582/14).

#### Processing

Any operation performed on personal data, whether automated or
manual, including collection, recording, organisation, structuring,
storage, adaptation, retrieval, consultation, use, disclosure,
alignment, combination, restriction, erasure, and destruction
(Art 4(2)). The definition is intentionally exhaustive; virtually
any handling of personal data qualifies.

#### Controller and processor

Functional determination, not contractual labelling: the controller
determines the purposes and means of processing (Art 4(7)); the
processor processes on behalf of the controller (Art 4(8)). The
concept applies broadly to "any body" that factually determines
purposes and means (_V.Q. v Land Hessen_, C-272/19; EDPB
Guidelines 07/2020). The CJEU has consistently held that factual
circumstances, not the parties' self-designation, determine the
roles (_Wirtschaftsakademie_, C-210/16; _Fashion ID_, C-40/17;
_Jehovan todistajat_, C-25/17). Joint controllership arises where
two or more entities jointly determine purposes and means, even
if their contributions and degrees of responsibility differ
(Art 26).

### Article 5 principles

The six principles in Article 5(1), combined with the
accountability principle in Article 5(2), form the analytical
backbone of every assessment.

| Principle                          | Article | Analytical function                                                                                                           |
| ---------------------------------- | ------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Lawfulness, fairness, transparency | 5(1)(a) | Requires a valid legal basis (Art 6) and honest, clear communication with data subjects                                       |
| Purpose limitation                 | 5(1)(b) | Processing must be for specified, explicit, and legitimate purposes; further processing must be compatible (Art 6(4) factors) |
| Data minimisation                  | 5(1)(c) | Only data adequate, relevant, and necessary for the stated purpose                                                            |
| Accuracy                           | 5(1)(d) | Data must be kept up to date; inaccurate data must be erased or rectified without delay                                       |
| Storage limitation                 | 5(1)(e) | Data kept in identifiable form only as long as necessary for the purpose                                                      |
| Integrity and confidentiality      | 5(1)(f) | Appropriate security measures against unauthorised or unlawful processing, accidental loss, destruction, or damage            |
| Accountability                     | 5(2)    | The controller must be able to demonstrate compliance with all principles                                                     |

### Jurisdictional scope

The GDPR applies in two situations (Art 3):

1. **Establishment criterion** (Art 3(1)): processing in the
   context of the activities of an establishment in the EEA,
   regardless of whether the processing itself takes place in
   the EEA.
2. **Targeting criterion** (Art 3(2)): processing of EEA data
   subjects' personal data by a controller or processor not
   established in the EEA, where the processing relates to
   offering goods or services to them (Art 3(2)(a)) or
   monitoring their behaviour within the EEA (Art 3(2)(b)).

The EDPB's Guidelines 3/2018 on territorial scope provide
detailed criteria for assessing both limbs, including the
factors for determining whether goods or services are "offered
to" EEA data subjects.

### Relationship between chapters

For assessment purposes, the regulation's chapters interact
as follows: Chapter II (principles) constrains all processing;
Chapter III (data subject rights) operationalises the principles;
Chapter IV (controller and processor obligations) implements
them organisationally; Chapter V (international transfers) adds
a layer for cross-border flows; Chapters VI-VII (supervisory
authorities and cooperation) provide the enforcement framework.
Each assessment prompt in this skill maps to one or more of
these chapters.
