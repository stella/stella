## Analysis patterns

### Tracing defined terms

Defined terms create the contract's internal vocabulary. Errors in their use are a common source of disputes.

#### Method

1. List all defined terms with their definitions.
2. For each defined term, search for every use in the operative provisions.
3. Check that each use is consistent with the definition. Flag instances where a term is used in a sense that differs from its definition, or where a term that should be defined is not.
4. Check for nested definitions (terms defined by reference to other defined terms) and resolve the full meaning. Circular definitions (A defined by reference to B, B defined by reference to A) are drafting errors.
5. Check for "orphan" definitions (defined but never used) and "undefined" terms (used with initial capitals suggesting a definition that does not exist).

### Assessing risk balance

Risk allocation is rarely symmetric. The analysis should map who bears each category of risk and whether the allocation is commercially reasonable given the parties' relative positions.

#### Method

1. For each risk-allocation mechanism (representations, warranties, indemnities, liability caps, force majeure), identify:
   - Which party bears the risk.
   - Whether the risk is capped or unlimited.
   - Whether the risk is insured or insurable.
2. Map the overall risk profile: is one party bearing disproportionate risk relative to its control over the relevant outcomes?
3. Consider whether the risk allocation is appropriate for the transaction type. For example: in a software license, the licensor typically bears IP infringement risk; in a construction contract, the contractor typically bears delay risk.

### Red flags

The following patterns warrant attention in any contract review:

- **Asymmetric termination rights.** One party can terminate for convenience while the other can only terminate for cause.
- **Uncapped liability.** A party faces unlimited liability without corresponding insurance requirements or indemnification rights.
- **Broad indemnification without baskets.** First-dollar indemnification obligations with no minimum threshold or deductible.
- **Vague performance standards.** Obligations to perform "to the satisfaction of" a party, without objective criteria, give one party unilateral discretion.
- **Missing survival clauses.** Confidentiality, indemnification, or limitation of liability provisions that do not survive termination, leaving a gap in protection.
- **Inconsistent governing law and dispute resolution.** A governing law clause specifying one jurisdiction and a dispute resolution clause specifying courts or arbitration in another, creating potential conflicts.
- **Unilateral amendment rights.** One party can modify terms (especially pricing, service levels, or scope) without the other's consent.
- **Automatic renewal without notice.** The contract renews automatically unless terminated by notice, but the notice period is unreasonably long or the termination window is narrow.
- **Change of control provisions.** Termination or consent rights triggered by a change of control may be too broad (capturing internal reorganizations) or too narrow (failing to capture indirect changes of control).
- **Waiver of jury trial or class action.** Provisions that may be unenforceable in certain jurisdictions or that significantly affect the parties' remedial options.
