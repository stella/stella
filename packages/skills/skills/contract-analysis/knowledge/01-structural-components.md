## Structural components of a contract

### Parties and capacity

- **Identification.** Full legal names, jurisdiction of incorporation or domicile, and the capacity in which each party acts (principal, agent, trustee, etc.).
- **Defined roles.** How the contract refers to each party (e.g. "Licensor," "Buyer," "Service Provider") and whether these definitions accurately reflect the commercial relationship.

### Recitals and background

- **Recitals (whereas clauses).** The factual and commercial background the parties have agreed as context. Recitals are not typically operative provisions, but they serve as evidence of the parties' shared understanding and inform the interpretation of ambiguous operative clauses.
- **Purpose statement.** The commercial objective of the transaction, which guides purposive interpretation of the agreement.

### Definitions

- **Defined terms.** Terms given specific meanings in the contract. These create a private language that overrides ordinary meaning within the instrument.
- **Consistency check.** Verify that defined terms are used consistently throughout the document. A term defined in one way but used differently elsewhere creates ambiguity.
- **Cross-references.** Definitions that incorporate other defined terms (nested definitions) can create complexity; trace the full meaning by resolving all layers.

### Operative provisions

- **Core obligations.** What each party must do (delivery, payment, performance of services, grant of rights). Distinguish between obligations (must do), conditions (must happen before an obligation arises), and discretionary rights (may do).
- **Performance standards.** How performance is measured: specific deliverables, service levels (SLAs), acceptance criteria, quality standards. Note whether standards are objective (measurable) or subjective (satisfaction of a party).
- **Timing.** When obligations must be performed: fixed dates, periods after triggering events, milestones. Note grace periods, cure periods, and the consequences of delay.

### Risk allocation

- **Representations and warranties.** Statements of fact (representations) and promises about the state of affairs (warranties) that allocate the risk of factual inaccuracy between the parties. Identify what each party represents, any knowledge qualifiers ("to the best of its knowledge"), and time limitations.
- **Indemnification.** Obligations to compensate the other party for specified losses. Note the scope (first-party vs. third-party claims), caps, baskets/thresholds, and exclusions.
- **Limitation of liability.** Caps on total liability, exclusions of consequential or indirect damages, carve-outs from limitations (e.g. for willful misconduct, IP infringement, confidentiality breach).
- **Insurance requirements.** Obligations to maintain insurance coverage and the allocation of insurable risk.
- **Force majeure.** Events beyond the parties' control that excuse performance. Note the definition's breadth, the notice requirements, and the consequences (suspension vs. termination).

### Temporality

- **Term.** The duration of the contract: fixed term, rolling/evergreen, or indefinite with notice-based termination.
- **Renewal.** Automatic renewal, renewal by notice, renegotiation requirements.
- **Termination.** Termination for cause (material breach, insolvency, change of control), termination for convenience (with or without notice period), and the mechanics of termination notices.
- **Survival.** Provisions that survive termination or expiration (confidentiality, indemnification, limitation of liability, governing law, dispute resolution).

### Dispute resolution

- **Governing law.** The substantive law that governs the contract's interpretation and performance.
- **Jurisdiction / arbitration.** Forum for dispute resolution: courts (exclusive or non-exclusive jurisdiction) or arbitration (institutional rules, seat, number of arbitrators, language).
- **Escalation.** Pre-litigation steps: negotiation periods, mediation, expert determination.
- **Interim relief.** Whether the contract preserves the right to seek injunctive or provisional relief from courts notwithstanding an arbitration clause.

### Boilerplate and miscellaneous

- **Amendment.** Requirements for modifying the contract (written form, signatures of both parties). Note whether the contract can be amended by conduct or course of dealing.
- **Assignment.** Whether rights and obligations can be transferred, and the conditions for transfer (consent, notice).
- **Notices.** How formal communications must be delivered (registered mail, email, specific addresses) and when they are deemed effective.
- **Severability.** What happens if a provision is held invalid or unenforceable.
- **Entire agreement / merger.** Whether the contract supersedes all prior negotiations and agreements. This affects the admissibility of extrinsic evidence for interpretation.
- **Waiver.** Whether failure to enforce a right constitutes a waiver of that right.
