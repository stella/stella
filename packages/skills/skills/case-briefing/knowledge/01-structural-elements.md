## Structural elements of a case brief

### Case identification

- **Case name and citation.** Full case name, court, date of decision, and official citation(s). Where multiple reporters exist, provide the most authoritative.
- **Panel composition.** Judges or justices who heard the case; identify the author of the majority opinion.

### Procedural history

- **Prior proceedings.** How the case reached this court: trial court disposition, appellate history, certiorari or leave to appeal.
- **Procedural posture.** What question is before this court: appeal on law, appeal on fact and law, constitutional reference, preliminary ruling, etc.

### Material facts

- **Facts the court relied on.** Distinguish the facts the court treated as material to its reasoning from background narrative. A fact is material if changing it would change the outcome.
- **Disputed facts.** Note any factual disputes and how the court resolved them (findings of fact, standard of review for factual determinations).

### Legal issues

- **Questions presented.** The precise legal questions the court addressed. Frame them as the court framed them, then note if the framing itself is significant.
- **Sub-issues.** Where a primary issue decomposes into subsidiary questions, map the structure.

### Applicable rules

- **Statutory provisions.** The specific sections, articles, or clauses at issue, quoted or closely paraphrased.
- **Constitutional or treaty provisions.** Higher-law norms invoked.
- **Prior case law.** Precedents the court applied, distinguished, or overruled.
- **Scholarly authority.** Doctrinal sources the court relied on (more common in civil law and international decisions).

### Reasoning chain

- **Step-by-step reconstruction.** Trace the court's reasoning from premises to conclusion. Identify the logical structure: deductive syllogism, analogical reasoning, policy-based reasoning, balancing.
- **Interpretive methods used.** Note which methods of interpretation the court employed (textual, purposive, systematic, historical) and how they interacted.
- **Key moves.** Identify the decisive analytical steps, the points where the reasoning could have gone a different way.

### Ratio decidendi vs. obiter dicta (common law)

- **Ratio.** The binding principle: the rule of law necessary to the decision, applied to the material facts. A case may contain multiple rationes if it decides multiple issues.
- **Obiter dicta.** Statements of law that are not necessary to the decision. These may be persuasive in future cases but are not binding.
- **Tests for distinguishing.** The ratio is what the court had to decide to reach its result; obiter is everything else. If the proposition could be removed without affecting the outcome, it is likely obiter.

### Syllogistic reasoning (civil law)

In civil law traditions, judicial reasoning characteristically takes a deductive form:

- **Major premise.** The applicable legal rule (statutory provision, codal article, regulatory norm).
- **Minor premise.** The characterization of the facts as falling within (or outside) the rule.
- **Conclusion.** The legal consequence that follows.

The brief should reconstruct this syllogism explicitly, even where the court's opinion does not present it in this form.

### Disposition

- **Holding.** The court's actual decision: affirmed, reversed, remanded, dismissed, etc.
- **Orders.** Specific relief granted or denied.
- **Scope.** What the decision settles and what it leaves open.

### Dissents and concurrences

- **Dissents.** The core point of disagreement: which premise (factual, legal, or methodological) the dissent rejects. Note whether the dissent proposes a different rule or applies the same rule differently.
- **Concurrences.** The additional or alternative reasoning offered. Note whether the concurrence agrees with the result but for different reasons, or agrees with the reasoning but adds a qualification.

### Variation across legal traditions

The relative weight of these elements shifts across traditions:

- **Common law:** Ratio/obiter distinction is central; reasoning is typically discursive and fact-intensive; individual judicial opinions (majority, concurrence, dissent) are separately authored.
- **Civil law:** Syllogistic structure is more prominent; opinions are often issued as a single institutional voice (the court's judgment, not individual opinions); legislative sources dominate over precedent.
- **International tribunals:** Treaty interpretation (VCLT Arts. 31-33) structures the reasoning; separate and dissenting opinions exist but the court speaks institutionally; prior jurisprudence is persuasive but not formally binding (though ICJ practice creates de facto precedent).
- **Mixed systems:** Combine elements; the brief should identify which tradition's conventions the court follows in the specific decision.
